## Deep Dive

### DeepMind Releases AGI Cognitive Framework: Finally, Someone Takes "What Counts as AGI" Seriously

**Source**: [Google DeepMind Blog](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/models-and-research/google-deepmind/measuring-agi-cognitive-framework/) | Paper: [Measuring Progress Toward AGI: A Cognitive Taxonomy](https://storage.googleapis.com/deepmind-media/DeepMind.com/Blog/measuring-progress-toward-agi/measuring-progress-toward-agi-a-cognitive-framework.pdf)

There's an uncomfortable silence in the AI field: everyone talks about AGI, but no one can clearly articulate what "achieving AGI" actually means. OpenAI's internal definition is "surpassing humans on most economic tasks"—that's a business metric, not a scientific standard. DeepMind's newly released cognitive framework attempts to provide a more rigorous answer from the perspective of cognitive science.

**Framework Core: 10 Cognitive Faculties**

The paper "Measuring Progress Toward AGI: A Cognitive Taxonomy" draws on decades of research from psychology, neuroscience, and cognitive science, proposing 10 key cognitive faculties for measuring AI systems' general intelligence:

1. **Perception**: Extracting and processing sensory information from the environment
2. **Generation**: Producing outputs such as text, speech, and actions
3. **Attention**: Focusing cognitive resources on important stimuli
4. **Learning**: Acquiring new knowledge through experience and instruction
5. **Memory**: Storing and retrieving information over time
6. **Reasoning**: Drawing valid conclusions through logical inference
7. **Metacognition**: Awareness and monitoring of one's own cognitive processes
8. **Executive functions**: Planning, inhibition, and cognitive flexibility
9. **Problem solving**: Finding effective solutions to domain-specific problems
10. **Social cognition**: Processing social information and responding appropriately in social contexts

The framework's greatest value lies not in which 10 abilities it lists—the cognitive science community has long agreed on these categories—but in its proposed **three-stage evaluation protocol**: first assess AI systems across tasks covering each cognitive faculty, then collect human baseline data from demographically representative samples, and finally map AI performance onto human capability distributions.

This "positioning relative to human distribution" approach is fascinating. It doesn't ask "how much can AI do," but rather "where does AI sit on the spectrum of human capabilities." This is a more meaningful comparison framework—similar to using height percentiles rather than absolute centimeters to assess child development.

**Why Now?**

Frankly, the timing is telling. Over the past few months, the pace of AI capability advancement has noticeably accelerated—Gemini 2.0 Ultra, Claude 4 Opus have debuted in succession, benchmark scores continue climbing, yet community trust in these scores is declining (data contamination, overfitting, and other issues are frequently questioned).

DeepMind's choice to release this AGI evaluation framework at this juncture serves two purposes: it establishes directional coordinates for its own research, while also sending a signal at the industry level: **the construction of evaluation infrastructure has become urgently necessary.**

More importantly, 5 of these 10 faculties are specifically identified as having the "largest evaluation gaps": learning, metacognition, attention, executive functions, and social cognition. This selection itself is a diagnostic report—current AI systems have relatively mature evaluations for perception, generation, and reasoning, but for higher-order cognitive abilities like "self-monitoring," "flexible planning," and "social understanding," we're essentially flying blind.

**Kaggle Partnership: Turning Framework into Tools**

A theoretical framework's value depends on whether it can be implemented. DeepMind's hackathon partnership with Kaggle cleverly solves this problem—using a $200,000 prize pool to invite the research community to **help build evaluation tools**.

The prize structure: top two in each of five faculty tracks receive $10,000 each; plus 4 best overall awards at $25,000 each. Submission window opens March 17 through April 16, with results announced June 1.

This strategy resembles open source community dynamics: rather than having internal teams handle all evaluation design, open the boundaries and introduce diverse perspectives. Cognitive capability evaluation is fundamentally an interdisciplinary problem—psychologists, education researchers, and linguists can all provide insights from different dimensions.

**My Take: This Is a Move, Not the Endgame**

I don't want to overstate this paper's significance. Cognitive taxonomy has its limitations—dividing human cognition into 10 independent dimensions is itself a simplification; AGI might emerge in ways completely unfamiliar to us, not necessarily needing to "exceed humans" across all 10 dimensions.

But more importantly, this framework does something right: **it pulls AGI back from marketing discourse onto the track of scientific discussion**. When AI companies race to announce "we've achieved AGI" or "we're close," a systematic evaluation framework grounded in cognitive science is an antidote to this rhetorical inflation.

The hackathon results are worth watching. If the community can produce high-quality evaluation benchmarks in metacognition and social cognition, we might see a batch of valuable AI capability reports in the second half of this year. That would be more meaningful progress than another round of MMLU score optimization.

## Week in Review

This is the last full week of March. Looking back, this week's AI news has a subtle throughline: **evaluation and measurement**.

DeepMind's AGI cognitive framework (today's headline) is the most direct manifestation. But this theme actually runs through the entire week: Anthropic's labor market impact report attempts to quantify AI's real economic effects; Claude Opus 3's deprecation represents iteration on capability baselines; Persona Selection Model touches on how AI systems understand and adapt to different interaction contexts—these are all different facets of "how to measure what AI is actually doing."

If 2024's keyword was "capability leaps," 2025 was "scale and cost," then early 2026's keyword might be becoming **"measurability"**—we don't just want to know what AI can do, we want to know how to verify it, compare it, and trust it.

This is a sign of maturation, and a necessary iteration.

Another signal worth recording this week is the "silence" from several major companies—OpenAI, NVIDIA, and Meta AI all had no major releases this week. This might just be normal release rhythm fluctuation, or it could be a charging period before bigger moves. Q2 is traditionally a dense release season for AI companies; the coming weeks are worth close attention.
