## Deep Dive

### OpenAI's Model Spec Explained: The Constitution Behind AI Behavior

**What happened**: On March 25, OpenAI published a detailed explainer, "Inside our approach to the Model Spec," offering the first systematic account of the design philosophy behind its [Model Spec](https://model-spec.openai.com/)—the formal framework governing how all OpenAI models behave.

**Background**:

The Model Spec itself isn't new; version one shipped in 2024. What's different this time is that OpenAI chose to explain *why* the spec is structured the way it is, not just *what* it says.

**Three top-level priorities (in order)**:
1. Iteratively deploy models that empower developers and users
2. Prevent models from causing serious harm to users or others
3. Maintain OpenAI's license to operate

Notice that "safety" is second, not first. "Empowerment" leads. This is a meaningful philosophical divergence from Anthropic, which puts harm prevention at the forefront. In practice, that ordering shapes where the behavioral guardrails land.

**"Models shouldn't autonomously pursue benefiting humanity"**:

One passage deserves a close read: OpenAI explicitly states that "benefiting humanity" is *OpenAI's* goal—not a goal they want models to pursue on their own. They want models to follow a chain of command: Model Spec + OpenAI rules + developer instructions + user instructions—even if the model "believes" it knows what's better for humanity.

This is a deliberate hedge against over-autonomous AI. As models become more capable, this principle carries far more weight than it would have two years ago.

**The political dimension of openness**:

OpenAI frames Model Spec as "democratically legible"—readable, debatable, and improvable by the public. Alongside this post, they also updated their [collective alignment](https://openai.com/index/collective-alignment-aug-2025-updates/) mechanism, inviting broader public participation in shaping AI behavior rules.

As AI systems embed deeper into social infrastructure, "what rules does AI follow?" is becoming a political question, not just an engineering one. OpenAI is choosing to get ahead of that conversation rather than be dragged into it.

### OpenAI Launches Safety Bug Bounty: Turning Researchers into Security Allies

**What happened**: Also on March 25, OpenAI launched a public Safety Bug Bounty program via Bugcrowd, targeting AI abuse and safety risks specifically—separate from its existing Security Bug Bounty.

**In-scope categories**:

- **Agentic risks (including MCP)**: Third-party prompt injection, data exfiltration attacks against Browser Agent, ChatGPT Agent, and similar products—reproducible at ≥50% rate
- **Proprietary information exposure**: Model generations that reveal reasoning-related proprietary data
- **Account and platform integrity**: Bypassing anti-automation controls, manipulating account trust signals

Three notable details:

1. **Jailbreaks are explicitly out of scope.** OpenAI appears to have accepted that some degree of jailbreaking is unavoidable; they want to focus effort on vulnerabilities that cause real, material harm.
2. **MCP is called out by name.** The Model Context Protocol for tool-calling is specifically named—signaling that OpenAI takes MCP supply-chain attacks seriously.
3. **It complements, not replaces, the existing program.** Safety Bug Bounty handles AI-specific abuse risks; Security Bug Bounty handles traditional security vulnerabilities.

Reward amounts aren't disclosed, which will determine how many top-tier researchers actually engage.

**Source**: [Original post](https://openai.com/index/safety-bug-bounty/)

## Anthropic

### Economic Research

**Anthropic Economic Index — March Report: Experienced Users Unlock Dramatically More Value**

Published March 24, this latest report from the Anthropic Economic Index answers a deceptively simple question: does using Claude longer make you better at using Claude?

**The answer is a clear yes, and the magnitude is striking**:

- Users with 6+ months of Claude usage achieve a **10% higher conversation success rate**
- Long-tenured users have **10% fewer personal conversations** and inputs reflecting **6% higher educational level**
- Users self-select models by task: among paying Claude.ai users, Opus is chosen **4 percentage points more** than average for coding, **7 points less** for tutoring

**Broader trends from the February 2026 data**:

- Use cases continue to diversify: the top 10 tasks accounted for **19% of traffic** in February, down from 24% in November 2025
- Approximately **49% of occupations** have had at least a quarter of their tasks touched by Claude
- Global usage inequality persists: the top 20 countries account for **48% of per-capita usage**—up from 45%

The report surfaces a key insight: early adoption advantages may be self-reinforcing. Effective AI use requires complementary skills that are acquired through practice. Those who started early may continue to widen the gap.

## Google DeepMind

### Research

**Measuring Progress Toward AGI: A Cognitive Framework (and $200K Kaggle Hackathon)**

Published March 17, DeepMind released "Measuring Progress Toward AGI: A Cognitive Taxonomy"—a scientifically grounded framework for assessing the cognitive capabilities of AI systems, paired with a Kaggle hackathon to build the evaluations.

**10 core cognitive abilities** identified as critical for AGI:

Perception, Generation, Attention, Learning, Memory, Reasoning, Metacognition, Executive functions, Problem solving, Social cognition.

**Three-stage evaluation protocol**:
1. Test AI systems across a broad task suite covering each ability, using held-out test sets
2. Collect human baselines from a demographically representative adult sample
3. Map AI performance relative to the distribution of human performance per ability

**The hackathon**: $200,000 prize pool via Kaggle's newly launched Community Benchmarks platform, focusing on 5 cognitive abilities with the largest evaluation gaps: learning, metacognition, attention, executive functions, and social cognition. Submissions open March 17 through April 16; results announced June 1.

DeepMind is doing something the field has long needed: not yet another benchmark, but a methodology for measuring AGI progress. In a world where everyone claims "AGI is near," having a rigorous framework to define what that even means is foundational work.

## OpenAI

### Company

**OpenAI to Acquire Astral: Python Toolchain Joins the Codex Ecosystem**

Announced March 19, OpenAI will acquire Astral—the team behind some of the most widely used open-source Python developer tools:
- **uv**: Ultra-fast dependency and environment management
- **Ruff**: The fastest Python linter and formatter available today
- **ty**: Static type checking enforcement

Current Codex metrics: **3x user growth** and **5x usage increase** since the start of the year; **2+ million weekly active users**.

OpenAI's strategic direction is clear: evolve from "code generation" to "participating in the entire development workflow"—planning changes, modifying codebases, running tools, verifying results, maintaining software over time. Astral's tools live directly inside that workflow.

OpenAI committed to continuing support for Astral's open-source products post-acquisition. Closing subject to regulatory approval.

## Trend Watch

**"How AI Behaves" Is Becoming the Defining Competitive Dimension of 2026**

The week's most significant signal: three of the top AI labs are nearly simultaneously making dense public statements about AI behavior norms.

OpenAI dissected Model Spec's design rationale. Anthropic showed us data on how AI is spreading through the labor market. DeepMind proposed a cognitive framework for quantifying AGI progress. Individually, each story matters. Together, they're transmitting the same message: **AI is becoming social infrastructure, and behavioral design is surpassing raw model capability as the core competitive axis**.

Two years ago, the competition was benchmarks and parameter counts. Now the deeper competition is: is your AI reliable, resistant to misuse, and capable of being audited?

The Astral acquisition is another signal worth watching: Codex is evolving from "code generator" toward "full-lifecycle software development agent." When AI participates across the entire development cycle, *trust* becomes a more critical attribute than *intelligence*.

That shift may be more consequential than any individual model upgrade.
