## Lead Story

### AI Is Compressing the Timeline of Scientific Research

**What happened**: Anthropic released four research articles within 48 hours, covering economic impact analysis, a new science blog launch, a technical tutorial on long-running AI tasks, and a first-person account from a Harvard professor.

**Context**: The volume alone is notable, but what matters more is the convergence. All four pieces point to the same theme: **AI as a research accelerator is transitioning from "potential" to "validated."**

The standout is Harvard physics professor Matthew Schwartz's "Vibe Physics" report. He used Claude Opus 4.5 to complete a genuine theoretical physics calculation — the resummation of the Sudakov shoulder in the C-parameter. This isn't a toy example; it's a formal paper now on arXiv (arXiv:2601.02484).

The numbers: 2 weeks, 110 draft versions, 36 million tokens. The traditional timeline for equivalent work: 1-2 years.

Schwartz offers a precise analogy: current AI is at "G2 level" — equivalent to a second-year graduate student. It can dramatically accelerate expert research but lacks a critical capability: "Taste" — the intuition for which research directions are worth pursuing.

This echoes what Fields Medalist Timothy Gowers said in the Anthropic Science Blog launch: "We're entering an era where AI greatly accelerates research but still needs us."

From a broader perspective, Dario Amodei's "Machines of Loving Grace" vision is materializing. His "compressed 21st century" is no longer just rhetoric.

**Links**: [Vibe Physics](https://www.anthropic.com/research/vibe-physics)

## Anthropic

### Research

**Economic Index Issue 5: Learning Curves**

February 2026 Claude usage data reveals several interesting patterns:

- **Diversification**: Top 10 tasks on Claude.ai dropped from 24% to 19% of total usage — users are discovering more use cases
- **Platform migration**: Programming tasks are shifting from Claude.ai to API (Claude Code growth is the main driver)
- **Learning curve effect**: High-frequency tenant users show 10% higher success rates than average, indicating proficiency matters
- **Model selection intelligence**: Users choose models based on task complexity — Opus usage increases for harder tasks
- **Geographic patterns**: US state-level usage is converging, but international gaps are widening

The most thought-provoking finding: early adopters may be both "most impacted" and "most benefited" by AI. This isn't a paradox — those who master the tool can create more value with it, while also becoming more dependent on it.

**Long-running Claude for Scientific Computing**

Siddharth Mishra-Sharma from Anthropic's Discovery team published a practical tutorial on running multi-day Claude Code tasks on HPC clusters.

Real case: Using Claude Opus 4.6 to implement a differentiable cosmological Boltzmann solver (clax). Work that typically takes months or years was completed in days.

Key techniques: test oracles, persistent memory, orchestration patterns. And an amusing detail — the "Ralph loop" pattern, specifically designed to prevent the agent from slacking off.

**Link**: [Tutorial](https://www.anthropic.com/research/long-running-Claude)

## Google DeepMind

**Measuring Progress Toward AGI: A Cognitive Framework**

DeepMind published a paper proposing a framework of 10 key cognitive capabilities for evaluating AGI progress: perception, generation, attention, learning, memory, reasoning, metacognition, executive function, problem-solving, and social cognition.

Alongside the paper, DeepMind partnered with Kaggle to host a hackathon with a $200,000 prize pool, focusing on five capabilities: learning, metacognition, attention, executive function, and social cognition.

This is an attempt to establish more systematic evaluation criteria for "AGI progress." Compared to single-point tests like "passing exam X," a cognitive capability framework at least offers a more comprehensive perspective.

**Link**: [Blog Post](https://deepmind.google/blog/measuring-progress-toward-agi-a-cognitive-framework/)

## Trend Watch

The clearest signal this week: **"AI for Science" is moving from proof-of-concept to actual output.**

Anthropic's four articles form a complete narrative arc:

1. Economic data shows users are learning to use AI more effectively
2. The science blog launch indicates company-level strategic investment
3. The technical tutorial provides replicable methodology
4. The physicist's practice report proves real-world results

This is no longer a discussion of "AI might transform research" — it's "AI is transforming research, and here's exactly how."

But note Professor Schwartz's sober assessment: AI is currently a "G2 grad student" — useful, but not independent. What it lacks — "Taste," the judgment of what problems are worth pursuing — remains human territory.

This may be the healthiest human-AI collaboration model right now: AI handles the heavy computation and iteration; humans handle direction-setting and quality control.
